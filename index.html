EAGLE: Smarter Perturbations for Trustworthy AI Explanations

How information-theoretic active learning can make post-hoc explanations more stable, sample-efficient, and uncertainty-aware.

Sumedha Chugh, Ranjitha Prasad, Nazreen Shah IIIT-Delhi arXiv:2603.14894
↓ Scroll to read

The Problem with Explaining Black Boxes

Modern machine learning models are incredibly powerful — but they're also opaque. As these models are deployed in high-stakes settings like criminal justice, healthcare, and finance, regulations such as the EU AI Act and GDPR increasingly demand that predictions come with meaningful explanations. Post-hoc explanation methods like LIME attempt to address this by fitting simple, interpretable surrogate models around a prediction of interest. The idea is intuitive: perturb the input, observe how the model's output changes, and learn which features matter locally.

But here's the catch — the quality of these explanations depends critically on which perturbations you sample. Current methods generate perturbations randomly or with simple heuristics, leading to explanations that can vary wildly across runs. Run LIME twice on the same input, and you might get two different stories about what the model is doing. This instability undermines the very trust that explanations are meant to build.

The Core Question

Can we design a principled, sample-efficient perturbation selection strategy that directly minimizes explanation uncertainty while preserving locality — leading to stable, trustworthy explanations?

This is the question our work, EAGLE (Expected Active Gain for Local Explanations), sets out to answer.

What is EAGLE?

EAGLE reframes perturbation selection for post-hoc explanations as an information-theoretic active learning problem. Instead of generating perturbations blindly, EAGLE iteratively selects the next perturbation that will maximally reduce uncertainty in the surrogate model's explanation parameters.

The framework builds on a Bayesian linear regression surrogate — the same kind used by methods like BayesLIME — but replaces the heuristic sampling strategy with a principled acquisition function derived from expected information gain (EIG).

The Acquisition Function

The key theoretical result of our work (Theorem 1) shows that maximizing the expected information gain in the Bayesian linear surrogate simplifies to an elegant closed-form acquisition rule:

EAGLE Acquisition Function
z* = arg maxz   πx₀(z) · z⊤Vϕz
Select perturbations that maximize locality-weighted posterior uncertainty.

This expression has a beautiful interpretation. The term z⊤Vϕz measures how much the candidate perturbation lies along directions where the surrogate parameters remain uncertain — exactly the directions where more data is most valuable. The locality kernel πx₀(z) ensures that we don't drift away from the neighborhood of interest. Together, they balance informativeness and locality in a principled way.

This is what distinguishes EAGLE from BayesLIME, which uses predictive variance as a proxy for uncertainty. BayesLIME's acquisition evaluates z⊤Vϕz but multiplied by the global scaling factor s², and crucially, does not incorporate the locality kernel when selecting new perturbations. EAGLE's explicit locality weighting ensures sampling stays focused on the relevant neighborhood.

Theoretical Guarantees

One of the strengths of EAGLE is that its principled derivation enables formal theoretical analysis — something rarely available for perturbation-based explainers.

Diminishing Returns on Information

We prove that the cumulative information gain under EAGLE's acquisition grows as O(d log t), where d is the feature dimension and t is the number of queries. This captures the intuition that early perturbations are enormously informative, while later ones contribute diminishing returns. The logarithmic scaling in t means that EAGLE efficiently allocates its query budget.

Sample Complexity

We derive high-probability bounds on the estimation error of the explanation weights, leading to a sample complexity result:

Sample Complexity
t = O( (d + log(1/δ)) / (κν²) )
Queries grow linearly with dimension d, logarithmically with confidence 1/δ, and quadratically with inverse accuracy 1/ν².

This tells us something practical: achieving higher precision demands quadratically more samples, but increasing the confidence level only costs a mild logarithmic penalty. These guarantees help practitioners decide how many queries are needed for a desired level of explanation reliability.

Empirical Results

We measured the pairwise Jaccard similarity of top-5 features across repeated explanation runs. EAGLE consistently achieves the highest or near-highest stability across datasets. While some baselines like Tilia perform competitively on specific datasets, EAGLE is the only method that maintains strong performance across both tabular and image domains.

Sampling Quality & Convergence

We measured D-efficiency (reduction in posterior covariance volume) and cumulative information gain (CIG) across the perturbation budget. EAGLE exhibits a steeper convergence trajectory from the very first acquisition steps, confirming that each perturbation it selects reduces uncertainty more efficiently than the baselines. The D-efficiency grows linearly in the plot (logarithmically in t), exactly as predicted by our Lemma 1.

Runtime Efficiency

Despite the active learning overhead, EAGLE is actually faster than BayesLIME. At N=500 on COMPAS, EAGLE takes 8.16 seconds versus BayesLIME's 14.56 seconds — a nearly 44% reduction. The acquisition function's closed-form nature avoids expensive optimization loops.

Tighter Uncertainty, Not Just Better Point Estimates

Beyond producing stable point estimates, EAGLE yields significantly narrower credible intervals on feature attributions. The 90% posterior credible intervals from EAGLE are consistently tighter than those from BayesLIME and BayLIME, meaning practitioners can more confidently separate genuinely important features from noise.

This is more than an academic distinction. In a recidivism prediction or a credit scoring model, knowing that "prior offenses" has an attribution of 0.15 ± 0.02 versus 0.15 ± 0.12 is the difference between an actionable explanation and a meaningless one.

Why This Matters

Post-hoc explanation methods are only useful if you can trust them. An explanation that changes every time you run it, or that doesn't tell you how confident it is, can be worse than no explanation at all — it creates a false sense of understanding.

EAGLE addresses this at a foundational level by asking: given a limited budget of black-box queries, how can we extract the maximum amount of information about what the model is doing locally? The answer — select perturbations that maximally reduce epistemic uncertainty while respecting locality — leads to explanations that are more stable, more sample-efficient, and accompanied by meaningful confidence estimates.

Key Takeaways

Principled acquisition: EAGLE derives a closed-form acquisition function from information-theoretic first principles, replacing ad-hoc heuristics.

Theoretical guarantees: O(d log t) information gain bounds and sample complexity guarantees — rare in the post-hoc explainability literature.

Consistent improvements: Higher stability, faster convergence, tighter uncertainty, and lower runtime across both tabular and image benchmarks.

Drop-in compatible: EAGLE uses the same Bayesian linear regression surrogate as BayesLIME, so the improvement comes purely from smarter sampling.

Looking Ahead

The EAGLE framework opens several exciting directions. Extending the acquisition function to batch-optimal strategies (beyond greedy top-B selection), applying it to non-linear surrogates, and integrating faithfulness metrics into the active learning loop are all promising avenues. We're also exploring how the information-theoretic perspective connects to broader questions about explanation evaluation and the relationship between sampling quality and explanation fidelity.

We believe that bringing the rigor of active learning and Bayesian experimental design to explainability is an important step toward making AI explanations worthy of the trust we place in them.
